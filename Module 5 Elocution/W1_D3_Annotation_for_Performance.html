Week 1, Day 3: Annotation for Performance

Project: Revolutionary Voice

Objective: Learn to annotate a text with performance markings for deliberate delivery.

Standards: ELA.11.C.2.1, ELA.K12.EE.6.1

💡 Key Concept: Professional speakers score their texts the way musicians mark sheet music. Every annotation is a conscious choice about how to deliver meaning.

📖 Lesson

Here is the annotation system you will use:

  • // = short pause (a beat)
  • //// = long pause (a breath, a moment)
  • CAPS = words to emphasize
  • [urgent], [measured], [defiant] = tone notes
  • Up/Down arrows = pitch rises or drops
  • (gesture) or (step forward) = physical blocking

Select a passage from Patrick Henry, Thomas Paine, or one of the Federalist Papers and annotate it for performance. This becomes your blueprint for the solo performance you will record later this week.

✅ Pro Tip
Start by reading your passage silently and marking only the ONE most important word in each sentence. Then layer in pauses and tone shifts around those anchor words.

🎯 Activities

📍 Checkpoint: Complete all activities below before moving to the next lesson.

🔬 Check Your Understanding

A speaker shifts from describing a problem to demanding a solution. Best annotation for the transition?

A. // before the demand, no tone change
B. //// before the demand, tone shift from [measured] to [urgent], key words EMPHASIZED
C. Speed up through the transition with no pauses
D. Whisper the entire demand
A major rhetorical shift deserves a longer pause, a tone shift annotation, and emphasis on key words of the new section.

✍️ Written Response

Choose a 4-6 sentence passage from Henry, Paine, or the Federalist Papers. Write it out with your performance annotations (use //, ////, CAPS, [tone] notes, etc.). Then explain your choices in 2-3 sentences.

✍️ Written Response

Write your Performance Statement (1 paragraph): Why these annotation choices? What should a listener feel at each key moment? How will your delivery bring out the author's rhetorical intent?
