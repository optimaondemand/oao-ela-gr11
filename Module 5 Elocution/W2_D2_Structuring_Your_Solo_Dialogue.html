Week 2, Day 2: Structuring Your Solo Dialogue

Project: American Voices in Dialogue

Objective: Arrange two texts into a 3-4 minute structured dialogue using ensemble techniques.

Standards: ELA.11.C.2.1, ELA.K12.EE.6.1

💡 Key Concept: A solo dialogue is not two readings stitched together. It is a conversation between ideas, created through vocal shift, physical shift, pause, and contrasting rhythm.

📖 Lesson

As a solo performer, use these techniques to make the dialogue clear:

Vocal Shift: Change tone, pace, and pitch when switching speakers.

Physical Shift: Look left for one speaker, right for the other. Or shift posture.

Pause Between Voices: Take a beat when switching. This signals the transition.

Contrasting Rhythm: One speaker fast, one slow. One in flowing sentences, the other in short punchy ones.

Call-and-Response: Arrange excerpts so one voice makes a claim and the other responds, counters, or deepens the idea.

✅ Pro Tip
Write each speaker's excerpts on different colored paper or in different colored ink. This helps you see the conversation structure at a glance during rehearsal.

🔬 Check Your Understanding

Solo Stanton vs. Millay. Best way to distinguish them?

A. Same voice and posture for both
B. Stanton: face forward, formal/measured (public). Millay: angled away, restless/intimate (private).
C. Shout both parts
D. Whisper both parts
Physical + vocal shifts give the listener two clear signals you have changed speakers.

✍️ Written Response

Write your arrangement: alternating excerpts with (1) text/lines, (2) vocal quality, and (3) transition technique for each. Aim for 6-10 sections totaling 3-4 minutes.

✍️ Written Response

Explain the conversation your arrangement creates. What theme connects the voices? How do they agree, clash, or build on each other?
