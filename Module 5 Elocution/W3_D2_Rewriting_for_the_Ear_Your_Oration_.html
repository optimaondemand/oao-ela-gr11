Week 3, Day 2: Rewriting for the Ear: Your Oration Draft

Project: Personal Oration

Objective: Write a complete 600-750 word oration draft adapted from your Module 4 essay.

Standards: ELA.11.C.2.1, ELA.11.C.1.5, ELA.K12.EE.5.1

💡 Key Concept: Adapting an essay for speech means real revision: shorter sentences, natural citations, spoken transitions, and a closing that moves the listener rather than just summarizing.

📖 Lesson

Sentence length: Shorter sentences land harder when spoken.

Repetition: In essays it is redundant; in speeches it is powerful. Think of Henry, Stanton, Douglass.

Citations: Weave naturally: "As Madison argued in Federalist No. 10..." not parenthetical references.

Transitions: Replace "Furthermore" with "Here is what that means today..." or "But there is another side to this..."

Counterclaims: Keep them. "Now, some would argue that..." then pivot with conviction.

Closing: End by moving the listener: call to action, lingering question, or connecting image.

✅ Pro Tip
After drafting, read your oration aloud. Every sentence that makes you stumble or run out of breath needs to be shortened or split.

🔬 Check Your Understanding

Your essay says: "Hamilton's defense of judicial independence in Federalist No. 78, wherein he characterizes the judiciary as the 'least dangerous branch,' establishes a foundational framework." How to adapt for speech?

A. Read as written
B. Cut entirely
C. Break into short sentences: "Hamilton called the judiciary the 'least dangerous branch.' He believed an independent court was liberty's last defense. That argument still shapes judicial power debates today."
D. "Hamilton liked judges."
Keep the content, break into short punchy statements. Listeners process one thought at a time.
